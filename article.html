Silicon Valley Bank's Troubles Spark Concerns for Tech Startup Market

NFT

Silicon Valley Bank is a major funder for tech startups and has suffered significant losses due to its close association with the tech industry. The bank was forced to sell $21 billion in securities at a $1.8 billion loss and received $500 million from General Atlantic. After a 75% surge in 2021, the bank lost two-thirds of its value in 2022 and plummeted 60% on Thursday. The Silicon Valley region is also struggling with a 30% decline in venture capital deal activity in 2022, and ongoing declines in valuations and IPOs suggest more difficulties in 2023.

SVB, known for supporting startups and providing traditional banking services, has faced a setback, which has raised concerns among its clients. Its recent financial maneuvers have exposed the fragility of the industry. Scott Orn, operating chief at Kruze Consulting, called SVB a "crown jewel" that he expects to survive and potentially get acquired by a bigger bank. However, this pullback could lead to an increase in borrowing costs for SVB's clients. SVB's mid-quarter update revealed that its clients' spending has decreased, with cash burn remaining high despite the slowdown in venture investing.

SVB had initially expected average deposits for Q1 to be between $171 billion and $175 billion, but revised the forecast to $167 billion to $169 billion. Clients are expected to continue burning cash at the same rate as in Q4 2022, according to the bank's mid-quarter update. Analysts at DA Davidson have expressed concerns that companies have not adjusted to the slower fundraising environment, while S&P and Moody's have both downgraded SVB's rating. Concerns have been raised about potential contagion effects, as clients may withdraw their funds and deposit them elsewhere. However, CEO Greg Becker has assured shareholders that the bank has ample liquidity and flexibility to manage its liquidity position. Mark Suster of Upfront Ventures has urged more VC community members to publicly express confidence in SVB.

SVB is relied on by Suster for funding high-risk, future-oriented ventures. Examples on the firm's website include loans to Sunrun, debt offerings to Built Robotics, and financing solutions for Saildrone. Despite facing credit challenges during the dot-com crash and financial crisis, SVB's loan losses remain low. Currently, analysts are more concerned with the deposit side of the business.

Despite concerns from analysts at Wedbush and a downgrade from Moody's due to high risk profile, SVB remains optimistic about its future. The bank pointed out that private equity and venture capital dry powder hit a record high in January, indicating that there is still plenty of cash available for startups. SVB hopes to continue to be a trusted source of financing for companies as they seek to access this capital.
